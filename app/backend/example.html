1. Running monthly business reviews

Q: Anyone have experience starting cross-functional monthly business reviews in their org? Operationally, how did you prep and run them? Any tips on the early phase building the momentum? Any good templates or posts you recommend?

– Tal Raviv

Chris Wilson: We’ve been running these for 2 years.  This is our template (slide 1):

Few highlights:

  • We are a marketplace company, so we are structure by supply, demand, and data with the flow by funnel/flywheel for this meeting. Essentially the AARRR framework.

  • Product or Business Owner drives these.  They have to run point and setup preps and push.

  • The core team is approximately 8 people across each department, so the company is represented.

  • Executive team is present at all these meetings (usually sponsor an area if they are deeply tied to it).

  • Metrics —> green/yellow/red to know where to focus —> Quick recap —> Top actions in each area between now and next MBR

  • Then we do a deep dive in any specific areas that have been requested or are red to show a plan or pivot

  • We’ve revised these a lot and they used to take up a ton of time so we’ve been cutting out the fluff and letting metrics be where we go with this meeting.  Therefore the metric identification and OKR setting is critical to have running otherwise it’s a mad dash. 

Tal Raviv: @Chris Wilson thank you this is soo good. Makes it incredibly concrete. How did you learn to run this - trial and error/experience? Any resources that you recommend?

Chris Wilson: Glad to hear it!  Complete trial and error on this one (so don’t have many but we’ve had a lot of ET members who have come from past experiences and brought a lot of the pieces to his). We’ve revised this so many times and cut it every which way but this format has been most well-received.  For the cross-departmental aspects, this has proven to be the best format and makes sense funnel-wise as well as splitting by supply/demand that everyone can grasp.  I can’t take credit for the current format as it’s been a lot of fine-tuning over the last several months.  Probably one of the most helpful meetings we have in the sense of the overall health of a business area and where to invest/bet more.

Archisman Das: At my previous org, we anchored the reviews around Business PnL and OKRs. Typically each of the OKRs has an owner assigned and in few cases, there are joint owners. There are initiatives attached to each of the KRs unless we're unclear on how to solve it in which case the first step is a research exercise.

The day before the review was spent doing lot of data deep-dives, reflecting on what we had worked on, what we had expected and what actually turned out happening and what are the next steps. We also shared our confidence level and wherever we could, forecasted where we would land by end of the quarter.

The Business Head took the lead in personally sharing the PnL update and I think that played a critical role in the rest of the team following his lead and putting rigor in their parts of the review. Also, during the initial stages helps in spending time defining a common language for the metrics. We also took the opportunity to recognize folks who did great work.

Tal Raviv: Btw to anyone following the thread, I found this gem from an MBR at Patreon, I saved the objective of the meeting at the time because I liked it so much:

 “The goal of this meeting is to build a shared understanding as a team about how our business is performing against plan. We should all walk out of the room with the same story about why our performance is above or below plan, and what we’re doing about it.”

2. Does the startup grind ever end

Q: When does the "startup grind" end? When do you start feeling like your head is above the water?

I had a discussion with a friend over the weekend about the feeling of always running at an unhealthy sprint in startups. We work at different companies, at different stages, with different funding sources. We both feel like we're running full speed but not running fast enough. The story of the kid damming the dyke with his fingers came up.

When do you think it ends? We came up with a few ideas

  1. When the hyper growth ends. Until then, there's no way to feel anything but the unhealthy sprint. Hyper growth is just not a healthy, sustainable state and as long as you're busy with that - whether because of pressure to fund raise or satisfy your own goals, - you'll be feeling that feeling.

  2. Until you reach cash flow positive/some other sustainable metric where your company can actually grow sustainably without needing to live by a VC 18 month "do or die" cycle.

- Yehoshua Zlotogorski

Michael Jelly: I get that. I feel like when you reach sustainability you have more freedom. You no longer are default dead.

That’s what we’re aiming for, I do feel kinda “back against the wall” until we make it. Ultimately I think there’s a self-perception thing here too - it’s important to feel like you’re running as fast as you can and not to feel like it’s not enough.

It’s definitely a struggle. 

Tal Raviv: One friend defined burnout for me as “feeling like you’re doing a lot but not seeing the correlating result”

Which to me (when I experienced that) is really never actually reaching sustainability or a milestone but still working super hard

I’m not sure if that’s the same situation but that’s what this reminded me of.

Nima Gardideh: It ends when you have a team you trust, where you can focus more on making better decisions based on the information they work on feathering for you instead of putting out fires. It can happen way earlier than what you've laid out, or never depending on what type of executive team you want to be running.

Yehoshua Zlotogorski: Thanks for the reply's all. It's an interesting topic. @Nima Gardideh I wonder when that happens for hyper-growth startups.

Nima Gardideh: I know founders that achieved it as early as Series B and as late as Series D/E ¯\_(ツ)_/¯. It's not really a formulaic thing – a lot of it is your own ability / emotional security to pass on responsibility to others you trust. I'd reckon it takes a lot of therapy to get there. Doesn't come naturally (at least to me).

We have integrations set up so that when I add feedback from a user around a feature, it copies to a Slack channel. It's easy to tag or assign a designer to an issue and he checks Linear regularly to see what users are saying. A teammate might see an easy request or fix I added and decides to do it.

Previously, a lot of the feedback was collecting in spreadsheets and notion docs that were less top of mind. If I were using a different but similar tool to Linear, I don't think it would work. So I'd possibly think about how easily whatever tool would fit into your workflow and the workflow of the people you want to see the customer feedback.

4. Prioritizing engineering work

Q: What are the best frameworks or mental models for prioritizing work for engineering teams?

Today, our product team uses GitHub issues to assign features, enhancements, and bugs to specific teams using labels. Those features are then bucketed into sequential (occasionally parallel) milestones. Each milestone is its own issue board in Github. Today, we are struggling with sequencing and getting everyone marching in the same direction (constant confusion of what to build now v. what to build next). We are not an 'Agile' company, so not looking to bucket everything into 2 weeks sprints. We are a 40+ person engineering team building B2B SaaS.

Curious if anyone has light-weight ways of doing this?

– David Kanter   

Karri Saarinen: We hope to share more around this. We are building Linear and we are also thinking about the frameworks too.

We don’t really follow agile either, but we think the 2-week sprints are useful to focus the team on what to do next. If the next priorities change every day or there are multiple priorities then it gets confusing. Essentially (using Linear here as an example but you could use some other tool too):

  • Have a quarterly roadmap of major projects (big blocks) that sets the direction and priority. Ideally, those roadmap items connect to some goals of the quarter. Prioritize them.

  • When you want to start on the projects, specify a team, the project team/owner writes a project brief and creates the project in Linear, let the team to scope the initial tasks. Project should be something that can be completed (like new features that will be shipped).

  • Then in backlog we would have bugs, smaller changes, as well as the new project work

  • Using 2-week cycles (our version of sprints), as a team you select what are the tasks you will focus on next. You probably want to prioritize the project work, but you can add a few bugs or fixes in the cycle as well (small blocks). We also assign all tasks so everyone commits to them. So then the priority for the team is just to focus on the tasks in that cycle and not think about anything else.

So the roadmap is the overall priority and direction, but the cycle defines what the team should focus on next, and it’s some kind routine/finite set of tasks, and not a constant stream of tasks or conflicting priorities. 

Simon Quick: I like using the ICE Score. it's low energy, decisive, and collaborative.

Useful for allowing Product/Engineering to collaborate on decision making as they're both coming from different viewpoints - e.g. Engineering most likely knows more about Ease, Product most likely knows more about Impact/Confidence. The Practical Advantage Of The ICE Score As A Test Prioritization Framework.

Aadil Maan: Shameless plug but I have written some thoughts on how planning happens at Apple and Google - Notes on Effective Release Planning

Now - tools - I was the Program Manager for Dart Language when I was working at Google; all open source so we did all bugs and planning in GitHub. It’s a terribly lacking tool for bug management or planning beyond the most basic thing.

With regards to prioritization - you don’t have to adopt a 2wks sprint cycle. I think it’s harder to do that with SaaS type companies or places where you are working on big features. For Dart, I got engineering to stick to a 6wks release cycle and that works. Prioritization of what to build next is an exercise in less tactical and more strategic. Do you use OKRs to establish a vision or roadmap? What about coming together to establish a quarterly theme? Ex Q1 is all about quality, let’s fix the Top 100 bugs. Q2 is about this new feature we constantly hear from our customers. Q3 is about this fantastic feature this engineer built in an internal hackathon.

Wayne Vernon: RICE is a nice variation on ICE: RICE Prioritization Framework for Product Managers [+Examples]

Karri Saarinen: Comment on the 2 weeks sprints, I don’t think how the industry matters as much, related more to other practices. I think SaaS companies are even more suited, since you likely ship constantly. We are a b2b SaaS company and ship new features and code almost every day, but some projects might take several weeks or months to complete. Even at Airbnb, the projects could take a year but we would cut and release the app every 2 weeks. In both places we had monorepo, where you keep merging to the master as often as possible and then you put features behind feature flags.

In our current company, we prefer smaller PRs, even for large projects. That way it’s easier to code review, test, merge and deploy behind a feature flag. I think a 6 weeks sprint can be fine too, but we just find it easier for people to think about how much of the project they can realistically achieve in 2 weeks. It doesn’t mean you need to complete the project in 2 weeks.  

Wayne Vernon: ☝️ Adding to that, I think it’s pretty common to decouple sprints and releases nowadays. Similar to Karri, we ship whenever we have things to ship. The sprints are really just providing structure to our planning and ceremonies, though we are much more scrum/kanban than proper agile.

Aadil Maan: I wonder and just a question to the group here, some amazing thought by the way, what do you qualify as “product is shipped”, if the feature will take several weeks to build but you are shipping chunks in 2wks cadence, what benefit are you gaining instead of just building it out in the time required? You have a feature when all the work is done which is longer than 2wks. Perhaps I am misunderstanding the process here.

Wayne Vernon: In most cases, it’s more about dev workflow vs. shipping value. Lower risk to ship code to production in smaller chunks than there is with waiting to do a big bang.

The other extreme is something like “Shape Up” where they claim to throw out anything that isn’t shipped by the end of the 6-week cycle. I have a hard time believing this happens very often in practice. 

Karri Saarinen: In addition to Wayne’s point, I’d say:

  • Lower risk of getting stuck in a technical or product path. As you make changes constantly with small PRs, which someone has to review, you also constantly communicate what kind of technical path you’re taking. Fellow engineers might see a better or easier path which could save effort.

  • We like to maintain momentum in the team on a weekly basis. I think all good teams I’ve been part to, always had this momentum of progress, you got something done every day and every week, and it felt exciting. This also helps everyone think how they can make progress now vs “thinking about it” over the weeks. Having the tasks in the cycle also communicates what they are doing to everyone else and they can follow the progress.

  • We collect the changes to a weekly changelog which also celebrates the momentum for us and the users Changelog

  • Lot of companies struggle (like OP mentioned) with changing and conflicting priorities. The 2-week cycle ideally creates a routine where you prioritize the work to be done only every 2 weeks, and create this protected time for the team to focus on the tasks in the cycle and ignore everything else (unless urgent?. After the cycle the situation resets and you can now prioritize whatever is important next. There is also an emotional benefit of having a finite list of things to do vs infinite, constantly changing list of things to do.

  • You can with the cycle, you can also communicate that you need someone’s help in the next 2 weeks and that way everyone can coordinate.

  • We think feature development as a workflow of refinement. The feature starts with a vague idea, solidifies to a brief, becomes a design, is architected and gets built in pieces and that way each part of the way we have the opportunity to improve the feature.

  • We ship new code multiple times a day but enable the features gradually. We start with enabling the feature just for our team, even when it’s in very early stages. This way everyone can start testing it in our own workflows and give feedback. Next stage we can reach out to some of the users who were interested in the feature and they can opt-in to the beta version through the app. Then we might release it to specific group of users (new users, existing users, customers of certain size) and eventually enable it to everyone.

Manuel Bruschi: @Karri Saarinen We start with enabling the feature just for our team, even when it’s in very early stages. How do you do that? Feature flags? Can I ask you about the tech stack there?

Karri Saarinen: @Manuel Bruschi yeah we use feature flags, and we have superusers flag which enables features for only our accounts. For the opt-in people can open our command menu and execute a command like “enable preview feature x”

Mostly typescript, react. Some more about stack in the Tech section of the job post https://linear.app/readme#engineer.

Dmitri Skjorshammer: @Karri Saarinen thanks for sharing. Out of curiosity, how do you maintain your Changelog? Are the changes pulled automatically from git commits or is someone summarizing the work weekly? 

Karri Saarinen: @Dmitri Skjorshammer yeah it’s manual. Someone just goes through all the PR merges last week and notes the interesting things. For the main features, we usually also have the person who builds it write the summary. It’s also one section in the project brief, so ideally it gets written first and then we can just publish with some modifications when we actually release the feature.

Luca Rossi: We have struggled a lot with that at Wanderio! Trying to find the right mix of long-term planning and short-term agility. I have written about our experience and what we settled with here: The Four Types of Work 🍀

TL;DR: we use multiple cycles to address different kinds of activities — a quarter cycle for planning and reviewing strategic initiatives (OKRs), a 2-week cycle to develop the above + unexpected opportunities, and a weekly fixed time allocated to maintenance (20%). Similar to @Karri Saarinen’s take. It is the most popular post of my newsletter 😅 sign that we are not the only ones struggling! 

5. Systems for getting feedback

Q: Has anyone implemented a process for employee feedback/complaints (separate from NPS or health check surveys)? I'm trying to put something together where employees can anonymously provide feedback or ideas for leadership at any time (vs when surveyed) to review/report back on some regular cadence. Is it worth doing this in addition to employee NPS?

– Annalee Bloomfield

Rachel Horowitz: Hey Annalee, we do this through an anonymous open-ended google form that goes out weekly which I think works well.

Annalee Bloomfield: Thanks @Rachel Horowitz! Do you guys ask any specific question in the form or just a general call for feedback?

Rachel Horowitz: Of course! It’s just a general “Do you have any questions or feedback for the leadership team”

Regan Davis: At a previous co, we used CultureAmp. It was helpful because they had specific questions and benchmarks to really understand by departments where we had gaps. It had notes fields next to each survey q. The challenge (as in all of this) was getting employees to actually provide feedback. But having a 3rd party tool made it more clear it was anonymous.

Matt Khoury: I like TinyPulse

Manuel Bruschi: Here are a few inspirations from what we’re doing:

  • We have half-yearly heartbeats where we go through a set of questions for feedback on various things, one of them is leadership, e.g. if you would be ceo what would you do differently?

  • We do anonymous surveys here and then. Questions vary, e.g. If you could change one thing about our culture what would it be and why? or Have you ever been in a situation at Timeular where you or someone else have felt uncomfortable, excluded, offended and/or did you encounter any racism or sexism? Please describe that situation. or we frame it like the product-market fit survey How would you feel if the company would not exist anymore? (Very disappointed, somewhat disappointed, not disappointed) & Please explain why? What are the main benefits for you working here? What would you 6 months from now like to see different or improved and why? Something else you would like to say?

  • A lot comes through the 1:1 and here we encourage all leaders to ask questions like: How can I be a better manager for you?, What advice would you give me if you were in my position?, If you were me, what would you do differently?… Those questions have the side-benefit that employees put themselves a few minutes in the shoes of leaders and creates awareness for the challenges they face.

4. How Apple is organized for innovation

Aadil Maan: This is definitely a great read: How Apple Is Organized for Innovation. I had a front row seat to what this entire functional structure is capable of as part of Apple’s iOS Program Office 2012 to 2017. It definitely worked but required discipline. I had worked under Sebastien Marieanu at both Blackberry and Apple.

Rostyslav Bohdanov: So Apple does not have cross-functional teams and classic PMs leading them?

Aadil Maan: They have cross functional teams but no PMs on the software engineering side (iOS, macOS, WatchOS, tvOS, iPadOS). They hire PMs but they work in more product marketing roles.

Rostyslav Bohdanov: @Aadil Maan - just to clarify - how does the project management done here? Who is in charge of specific projects- like expert VPs? How does cross functional teams are assembled? It’s like VP comes up with project - and a team from different departments assembles to make the progress with this project? Or I miss something?

Aadil Maan: Oh boy, this is going to be a long response. Bare with me - Apple has a central Project Management team called the Program Office. The VP reports directly to Craig Federighi. I heard rumors that now the Program Office reports to Sebastien Marieanu. When I joined, the iOS program office was the original iPhone team project managers. When Scott Forstall left, Craig absorbed both iOS and macOS and the two program offices merged. In some cases the functional organizations may have their own teams of project managers. The Program Office project managers were the DRI EPMs and were responsible for large cross functional features from E2E (requirements, planning down to deployment and maintenance). They worked with the individual team project managers.

What happened was that all the SVPs (Craig, and Eddy along with all the VP experts would evaluate research and roadmaps from all the functional teams, design, product marketing, hw) and decide on themes - like better ipad productivity or Apple Pay expansion.

When the themes are decided, the VPs then propose features underneath these themes - like handwriting recognition in notes, or expand to Japan for Apple Pay. There is always one team that has the most skin in the game and often times it would be the UI teams like Apple Pay app client team or Notes App team. The manager from that team would be the designated Eng DRI (Directly responsible individual). They will have accountability to ensure they collaborate with all the dependent teams needed to ship the feature. Each feature is assigned an EPM (Engineering Project Manager) DRI - this was my role - to work alongside the Eng DRI.

There would be DRIs assigned from Product Marketing, Design, and QA for each feature. However, the ultimate 1st line decision making was Eng and EPM DRI. Each feature will have a VP exec sponsor; they are the 2nd line of decision making and what the discretionary leadership mode talks about in the article. When I joined back in 2012, the DRIs would work directly with Craig and Scott and Eddy but as the product and features expanded in iOS and macOS, then watchOS, the exec sponsor model was introduced to increase decision making speed.

The final decision makers ultimately are Craig and the SVPs. They are the product managers per say. Now, the Eng and EPM DRI are responsible for identifying the key dependency teams, assembling the cross functional team, develop engineering solutions and project plans to ship the feature by WWDC and then OS release in September. They get sign offs from each functional team's VP and coordinate alignment with the exec sponsors and have regular checkins with the SVPs on progress.
